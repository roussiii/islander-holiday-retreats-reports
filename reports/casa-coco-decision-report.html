The two options

  • Long-term rental — ₨85,000 per month, fixed. The tenant pays their own electricity, water and wifi.
  • Short-term rental — the villa earns per stay. The owner pays the electricity, water and wifi.

For a fair comparison, all bills are therefore deducted from the short-term side before any figure is compared, together with the ₨4,000 paid to the cleaning company per stay. Everything below is calculated that way.

Reading the months

  • August & September 2025 — the launch: a brand-new listing with zero reviews. With 12 reviews now in place, this starting position no longer exists.
  • December & January — read them together: the New Year stay (10 nights at the year's highest rates) checks out on 1 January, so it is paid in January's statement. December alone looks small; December + January together paid ₨279,107 — more than three months of rent — and January's statement, ₨229,925, is the best of the year.
  • February and March — short by ₨3,693 and ₨734: effectively draws, with summer electricity fully counted.
  • May and June — the genuine quiet season. October, November, January, April and July earned the difference back several times over; the yearly total above already includes it.
  • Still on the table: January's calendar had unsold peak nights even in its record month. The 2026-27 pricing programme (adjusted floors, relaxed minimum stay, earlier visibility) targets exactly that upside.

And if summer electricity runs high? Last season's four peak statements (November – February) paid ₨500,967 after cleaning, against ₨340,000 of rent for the same four months. Stress-testing the summer bills:

  • At ₨30,000 of electricity & water every summer month — well above anything billed to date — the peak window still beats the rent by ≈₨55,000.
  • Even at ₨40,000 every summer month, it still wins, by ≈₨25,000.
  • The peak months would only stop beating the rent if bills exceeded ≈₨48,000 every single summer month. The season carries its own bills — and more.
